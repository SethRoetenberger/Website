Jellyfish Lights

I began working on this light by modeling it in Fusion 360. There are multiple organic parts to the model, so it was a challenge to design it for 3D printing. I also designed the internals to accept a battery pack to keep the jellyfish wireless. To do this, I created a battery holder that slid into the bottom and twisted and locked into place to securely hold the batteries. To make the outer shell and tentacles of the jellyfish, I printed them out of a clear TPU. This allowed them to be flexible and see-through. I heated up and bent the tips of the tentacles so that they could be clamped to the body. Once the modeling of the assembly was finished, I moved on to the electronics. I used an ESP32 and a voltage regulator to properly control the lights. I then created two more jellyfish. After a couple of uses, it became clear that the batteries did not have enough amp hours to drive the LEDS for a long period of time. I had done the calculations on this, but it did not match up with reality.

To solve this new issue, I decided to make the jellyfish wired. The batteries were removed, and I made a hanging device for all 3 jellyfish to connect to which included a touch sensor imbedded in the wood. I simplified the electronics so that only one ESP32 was required and each jellyfish used one output pin.
